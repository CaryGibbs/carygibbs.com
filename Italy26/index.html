Driving & parking in Italy

  • ZTL zonesHistoric centres — Tropea, Pizzo, Scilla — are ZTL (limited-traffic zones: resident/permit only), marked by a round red-bordered sign with a camera at the entrance. There's no barrier, but drive in without a permit and the camera issues a fine (~€80+) that reaches you weeks later via the hire company. So don't follow the sat-nav into the old town — park just before the ZTL and walk in (centres are small, 5–10 min).
  • Parking linesBlue = paid: pay at the parcometro or an app, keep the ticket on the dash (~€10–15/day in peak beach spots). White = free (rare). Yellow = reserved — don't use. The villa's own parking and the beach lidos near Zambrone are outside all this.

Zambrone — eat & shop

Zambrone is a small village, so the standout eating and the real shopping are a short hop away in Tropea and Pizzo. For the everyday, there's enough on the doorstep — and the diving off Capo Vaticano is the best on this coast.

Eating nearby

  • SeafoodFish Food & Fond of the Sea (Contrada Lampasi) — tiny family place serving only what was caught that day; widely called the best fish meal in Calabria. Book ahead.
  • PizzaPizzeria Bidreams (Via del Mare) — wood-fired pizza you can eat on a sunlounger as the sun drops behind Stromboli.
  • BeachLido La Bianca and Sole e Mare — sea-view lidos for easy lunches, drinks and bites.
  • GelatoBar Gelateria La Marinella (by the station) — the local favourite for gelato and coffee.
  • Big nightsSave these for Tropea (seafood + the Corso) and Pizzo (tartufo on Piazza della Repubblica).

Shopping & supplies

  • Daily basicsAl Mio Market (Via Quasimodo) — friendly, well-stocked, and will make beach sandwiches to order. The pick for everyday.
  • Also handyMarket Alimentari (Via Marina, fresh bread daily) and Supermercato Venere (SS522). Most shops close ~13:00–17:00.
  • Weekly stock-upConad at Ricadi / Santa Domenica (~15 min, toward Capo Vaticano) — a proper supermarket.
  • BrowsingFor clothes, gifts and souvenirs, Tropea's Corso Vittorio Emanuele is the strip — best in the early evening once it cools.

Calabria → Rome — the drive, via Pompeii

Sunday 19 July is the road-trip day, broken at Pompeii — it sits right on the route, just off the A3 between Salerno and Naples. An early start keeps it relaxed: ruins by late morning, then on to Rome to drop the car with time to spare.

  • ~07:30Leave Zambrone after breakfast. A2 north, toll-free through Calabria.
  • ~11:15Arrive Pompeii (~380 km). Park at Porta Marina / Piazza Esedra.
  • 11:30–14:30Ruins + lunch. Highlights: the Forum, House of the Faun, Stabian Baths, the Amphitheatre, the plaster casts. Allow ~3 hrs — it's vast, with little shade.
  • ~14:45Back on the road — tolled A1 to Rome (~€25–30 tolls all-in, card accepted).
  • ~17:30Fuel up, drop the BMW at Fiumicino (Sixt — due by 21:00, so comfortable). Taxi to the aparthotel (~€55, ~40 min).
  • EveningCheck in near the Vatican, relaxed dinner. Sightseeing starts properly tomorrow.
Pompeii tickets — booked. All four hold the 09:00 morning band (enter any time 09:00–13:00, so your ~11:15 arrival is fine). Cary & Catherine full (€20 each), Abi & Emily free under-18. Print on A4 and keep them on your phones, and bring all four passports — the free tickets are ID-checked. Single entry only; no large bags or umbrellas at the gate (free cloakroom). Hats, water and proper shoes — July is fierce and shade is scarce.

Pompeii — making the most of it

  • PrepSturdy closed shoes for all four — the paving is uneven and ankle-turning, no sandals. Hats, high-factor sunscreen, a refillable bottle each (fountains inside to top up). Take only a small bag; anything over 30×30×15 cm goes in the free cloakroom, so leave the rest locked in the car with nothing on show.
  • TimingYour band is 09:00–13:00 — you must be inside by 13:00. Earlier is cooler and quieter (tour groups pour in late morning), so leave Zambrone promptly. July tops 38°C with almost no shade, so a strong ~3 hrs is plenty before the heat wins.
  • At the gateEnter at Porta Marina, all four passports in hand (tickets are name-checked). Grab the free map and pick a route. Download the free Rick Steves Pompeii audio tour to your phones beforehand — it brings the place alive far better than the signage (the official app is flaky).
  • PrioritiesIn ~3 hrs aim for the Forum (Vesuvius behind it), the plaster casts at the Garden of the Fugitives, the restored House of the Vettii, the House of the Faun, the Stabian Baths, a thermopolium (ancient fast-food bar) and the Lupanar (the little brothel — do it early before the queue builds).
  • For the girlsIt's the human detail that lands: chariot ruts worn in the stone, raised stepping-stones, the "Cave Canem" dog mosaic, scratched graffiti, and the body casts — a city frozen mid-morning. A "spot the…" challenge keeps them moving.
Check before the day. A day or two ahead, glance at pompeiisites.org news for live closures — through July the Amphitheatre is set up for summer concerts and may only be viewable from the far southern end, and the odd house shuts for maintenance. Single entry only, so it's one continuous visit; eat outside the gate in Pompeii town rather than the hot midday site cafés.
Fuel before you drop. Top up near Fiumicino airport so the car goes back full and you dodge Sixt's refuelling charge. Photograph the car at handover.

06

The places, in brief

A quick why-it's-worth-it on each stop — what makes it tick and the family angle.

Calabria

Tropea. Voted Italy's most beautiful village: a cliff-top old town of pastel lanes above turquoise water, crowned by the Santa Maria dell'Isola church on its own rock. Easy wins for all four — gelato and people-watching for the girls, a showstopper sunset, and a beach right below. Famous for the sweet red onions you'll see in everything.

Capo Vaticano. One of Italy's most dramatic coastlines — white cliffs dropping into electric-blue coves. The Belvedere viewpoint is a five-minute stop with a huge payoff (Stromboli on the horizon); the hidden beaches below are a snorkelling and boat adventure. It's also your diving base.

Pizzo. A pretty clifftop town with a little seafront castle and one big claim to fame: it's the birthplace of tartufo, a chocolate-bombed ice-cream ball. Compact, walkable, and built around eating gelato by the sea — an easy, happy afternoon.

Zungri caves. A "mini-Matera" of ~100 cave dwellings carved into the rock, with a small museum and a self-guided walk. Shadier and cooler than the beach, genuinely intriguing for kids (people actually lived in these), and cheap — a good change-of-pace morning.

Scilla. A storybook fishing village (Chianalea) of colourful houses rising straight from the sea beneath a castle, tied to the myth of the monster Scylla from the Odyssey. The bigger day out (~1 hr south), rewarded with alleys to the water and a famous swordfish lunch.

Aeolian Islands. A hydrofoil hop to volcanic islands offshore — including Stromboli, which smokes and glows for real. A proper expedition day of black-sand beaches, clear swimming and a live volcano: the standout adventure of the Calabria leg.

The boat day. Hiring a small boat (no licence needed) or a guided tour is the only way into Capo Vaticano's secret coves and sea caves. Swimming off the boat in glass-clear water, with the old town seen from the sea — a highlight in its own right, not just transport.

On the road

Pompeii. An entire Roman city frozen by Vesuvius in 79 AD — streets, shops, houses, baths, even plaster casts of the people caught in the eruption. It makes ancient history suddenly real in a way no book can, and it's the perfect primer for Rome two days later. Vast and shadeless, so go early with hats and water.

Rome

Colosseum, Roman Forum & Palatine. The giant arena where gladiators fought, beside the ruined heart of the ancient city. Far bigger and more astonishing in person than on screen; your timed ticket walks you through the same tiers the Roman crowds did, with the Forum and Palatine filling in the city around it.

Vatican Museums, Sistine Chapel & St Peter's. Corridors of maps and Raphael frescoes leading to Michelangelo's Sistine ceiling, then the vast St Peter's Basilica. Your guided tour means it's explained rather than just gawped at — much better for keeping the girls engaged. Optional dome climb for the best view in Rome.

The old centre — Pantheon, Navona, Trevi & Spanish Steps. A walkable cluster of Rome's greatest hits: a 2,000-year-old temple with an open dome, a baroque square built over a chariot stadium, the coin-toss fountain, and the famous steps. Best strolled in the cool of the evening with gelato in hand.

Castel Sant'Angelo. A round riverside fortress that began as an emperor's tomb, reached over a bridge lined with stone angels. A short, fun visit with ramparts to roam and a terrace view straight to St Peter's — a natural stop on the walk back from the Vatican.

Villa Borghese. Rome's great green park — shaded, calm and far cooler than the centre, with rowing boats, bike hire and gardens. The perfect antidote to two days of crowds and stone, and an easy roll downhill to the Spanish Steps.

Trastevere. The atmospheric old quarter across the river — ivy-clad lanes, lively piazzas and the city's best casual trattorias. Where you'll have your Roman pasta dinner; come hungry and a little early to beat the queues.
